Regenerative Cooled Engine Development

A structured propulsion design process — from isentropic theory, to NASA CEA performance modeling, to contour generation and CFD validation.

Step 1 — Ideal Isentropic Framework

The engine design began with ideal isentropic flow relations to establish theoretical performance bounds. Using chamber pressure and expansion ratio targets, I derived exit Mach number, temperature ratios, and pressure distributions.

These equations provided the theoretical baseline before introducing real-gas effects.

Step 2 — NASA CEA Performance Modeling

After ideal approximations, NASA CEA was used to incorporate chemical equilibrium and real combustion effects.

Chamber temperature, specific impulse, gamma variation, and optimal mixture ratio were evaluated across operating conditions.

This refined thermodynamic properties used in contour generation.

Step 3 — Contour Generation & CFD Validation

SHOCKWVE, A contour generating tool was then used to generate the nozzle contour based on updated thermodynamic properties.

The resulting geometry was validated using CFD to ensure smooth expansion, minimized flow separation, and uniform exit conditions.

CFD confirmed pressure distribution and flow angle alignment within expected theoretical tolerances.

Regenerative Channel Thermal Design

Step 1 — Gas-Side Thermal Loading

The thermal analysis begins by evaluating the gas-side heat flux distribution along the chamber and throat.

Convective heat transfer coefficients on the gas side (hg) are estimated to determine local heat flux into the chamber wall, with peak loading occurring near the throat region.

This establishes the boundary condition for regenerative cooling design.

Step 2 — Channel Geometry & Coolant Flow Modeling

Using the HEATWVE program, a Regenerative channel geometry is defined at the throat and along the chamber using hydraulic diameter, cross-sectional area, and coolant mass flow rate.

Coolant pressure drop is tracked from channel inlet to injector interface, ensuring adequate injector feed pressure while maximizing heat removal.

The coolant-side heat transfer coefficient (hc) is monitored to ensure:

hc > hg

This condition guarantees effective thermal protection of the chamber wall.

Step 3 — Integrated Thermal Performance Modeling (HeatWve)

Regenerative geometry, gas-side heat flux, material properties, and coolant flow parameters are input into HeatWve — a Python-based thermal solver developed to evaluate engine cooling performance.

Key Performance Outputs:

  • Inner wall temperature distribution
  • Coolant pressure drop to injector interface
  • Gas-side vs coolant-side heat transfer coefficient comparison
  • Margin to material temperature limits

Channel geometry is iteratively adjusted until wall temperatures remain within allowable limits while maintaining acceptable pressure drop.
